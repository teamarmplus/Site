What your Site Check result
may mean

The free Site Check shows what we found from official government and open data sources. This page explains what those findings may mean — and what is still missing before you make any property decision.

How to read your result

What we found is a list of the data we could confirm from connected official sources. This may include the address, council or LGA, approximate land size, zone, and any overlay or hazard indicators connected for your state.

Not all states have the same depth of data connected yet. Where data is missing, we say so clearly rather than guess.

What this may mean is a plain-English explanation of what the confirmed data may indicate about the land. It is not a valuation, planning certificate, or advice of any kind. It is context to help you ask better questions before spending money on professionals.

What is still missing is a list of things that automated data sources cannot confirm. These are the checks that need a licensed professional to verify properly before any important decision.

What the zone may mean

A zone code tells you what category of land use applies under the local planning scheme. For example, a residential zone may allow certain types of housing but not others. Zones do not confirm what is permitted — they are a starting point. Local council development controls, minimum lot sizes, and overlays all affect what is actually possible.

If the zone was not confirmed, it means the connected data source did not return a result for that address. This may be because the state's planning data is not yet connected, or the address needs verification.

What an overlay or hazard indicator may mean

Overlays such as heritage, flood planning area, or bushfire prone land do not necessarily stop development or sale. They mean that any proposed change to the property will need more assessment, more time, and possibly more cost.

If an overlay was detected, do not assume it prevents your plans — but do not ignore it either. Ask a licensed town planner what it means for your specific situation.

What the land size may mean

The approximate land size from the Site Check comes from official cadastral (land boundary) data where connected. It may differ from the title area, especially for rural, steeply sloped, or irregular lots.

Land size matters because most councils have a minimum lot size that determines whether subdivision is possible. If the land is near or above the minimum lot size for the zone, a professional assessment may be worthwhile.

What is still missing from every automated check

Important: No automated check can replace a proper professional assessment. The following items are not included in the free Site Check and must be verified by a licensed professional before any property decision.

  • □Survey and boundaries — the exact legal boundary of the land, including frontage width, depth, and any encroachments
  • □Easements and encumbrances — rights or restrictions on the title that may affect what you can do with the land
  • □Drainage and stormwater — how stormwater leaves the site and whether infrastructure exists or needs to be added
  • □Access and driveway — whether legal vehicle access exists and what works may be required
  • □Services connections — sewer, water, power, gas — whether connected or what connection costs may apply
  • □Slope, retaining and earthworks — whether the slope of the land affects what can be built and at what cost
  • □Trees and environmental constraints — significant trees, vegetation, or environmental overlays not detected in the automated check
  • □Local council DCP controls — development control plans that add conditions beyond the zoning code
  • □Contamination and acid sulfate soils — not assessed in the automated check; requires environmental investigation
  • □Title review — a full title search to confirm ownership, encumbrances, easements, and caveats
  • □Professional valuation — a registered valuer's formal assessment of the land's value

Why professional verification matters

Property decisions are often among the most expensive and important decisions a person makes. Automated data checks give a useful starting point — but they are not a substitute for a licensed town planner, surveyor, solicitor, civil engineer, or registered valuer reviewing the specific land.
